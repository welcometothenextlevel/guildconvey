Victorian Property Law

What Makes VIC Conveyancing Different

Victoria has some of the most detailed conveyancing requirements in Australia. Every vendor must provide a Section 32 Vendor Statement before a contract can be signed — it discloses everything a buyer needs to know about the property, from title details to planning information and council rates.

Buyers in Victoria have a 3-business-day cooling-off period after signing a contract of sale. Our team will guide you through your rights and ensure you never miss a critical deadline.

Standard settlement in Victoria is 30–60 days. Guild Conveyancing handles every step electronically via PEXA — no paper, no office visits, no delays.

VIC FAQ

Victoria Conveyancing Questions

A Section 32 (also called a Vendor Statement) is a legal document the seller must provide before you sign a contract in Victoria. It discloses title details, planning information, rates, owners corporation fees, and any encumbrances on the property. Guild prepares Section 32s for sellers and reviews them thoroughly for buyers.

In Victoria, buyers have 3 clear business days after signing a contract of sale to cool off. If you withdraw, you forfeit 0.2% of the purchase price. Cooling-off does not apply if you buy at or within 3 days before or after a publicly advertised auction.

Victorian first home buyers can access a full stamp duty exemption on properties valued up to $600,000, with a concession on properties up to $750,000. The First Home Owner Grant of $10,000 is also available on new homes up to $750,000. Our team will identify every concession you qualify for.

Both licensed conveyancers and solicitors can handle residential conveyancing in Victoria. Guild uses qualified conveyancing professionals and property solicitors depending on the complexity of your transaction. For straightforward residential purchases and sales, a licensed conveyancer is perfectly appropriate.
